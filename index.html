About The Workshop

Welcome to the first international workshop on Quantum Resource Estimation (QRE). Co-located with the ACM International Symposium on Computer Architectures (ISCA), QRE is focused on the emerging field of Quantum Resource Estimation, benchmarking and performance analytics. The main objective of QRE is to organise a forum where researchers in acedemia and industry can share research on the near term feasibility of interesting scientifically and/or commercially useful quantum algorithms by shifting the perspective from complexity theoretic arguments to quantitative computer architecture arguments. The workshop proposes a top down approach, from high level algorithmic construction and optimization down to the actual implementation on hardware systems being constructed worldwide.

Multiple factors influence the amount of physical resources needed for executing a quantum algorithm. Physical resources can vary significantly due to the structure and designed precision of a high level quantum algorithm and the resultant quantum circuit, while additional overhead is introduced due to the physical constraints of the quantum hardware and the introduction of resource hungry quantum error correction. Even the design and the performance of the classical control software that compiles algorithms and controls the quantum computer has a non-negligible impact on the amount of physical resources needed for implementation of a given program.

The possibility of NISQ machines sparked the interest of exact, realistic and non asymptotic resource estimations of computations, driven by the recent introduction of multiple, small-scale, cloud-based quantum computing platforms. However, it is still very uncertain if any commercially or scientifically valuable quantum algorithm that outperforms classical supercompuers (either in a theoretical or monetary sense) is possible without incorporating costly error-correction protocols that make resource estimation, benchmarking and optimization far more complex.

This workshop is focused around developing techniques and tools that aid quantum software and algorithm design, informed by the realities of the hardware architectures. We hope to encourage participation from those working in quantum algorithm optimization, error-correction, architecture design, quantum compilation, classical control and resource benchmarking to help reduce physical resource costs for interesting quantum algorithms as quickly as possible.

Submission Site

Quantum computation has a growing number of promising application areas such as quantum chemistry, quantum optimisation and finance. However, the first industrially relevant and scalable quantum computer seems to be at least a decade away. Therefore, one of the most pressing questions is "How many physical qubits and how much time is necessary to execute a quantum algorithm on a selected hardware platform where the algorithmic output is more important than the the fact a quantum computer was used to calculate it?" By examining this question in depth we can motivate continued investment for quantum computing, further enable resource friendly quantum algorithm development and continue to push technological advances that will lead to a scalable quantum computing ecosystem.

The workshop will bring together researchers to discuss new methods and directions needed to develop the tools to accurately analyze and benchmark complex quantum algorithms, adapt error-correction techniques and refine classical control and hardware microarchitectures to enable scientifically and commercially relavant quantum applications as soon as possible.

Research papers, tutorials, software and other demonstrations, and work-in-progress reports are within the scope of the conference. Invited talks by leading international experts will complete the program. Contributions on all areas of quantum performance analytics are welcome:

  • High level quantum circuit analytics.
  • Fault-tolerant quantum circuit analytics.
  • Clifford+T optimisation strategies.
  • Resource efficient surface code implementations.
  • Surface code decoders.
  • Practical quantitative analysis of surface code alternatives.
  • Noisy Intermediate Scale Quantum (NISQ) evaluation.

Initial submission for QRE2019 will consist of an extended abstract, limited to 2+epsilon-pages (including figures and references, please don't go nuts with the epsilon!). Upon acceptance, researchers are invited to submit full research papers (maximum 12 pages), as well as work-in-progress or tool demonstration papers (maximum 6 pages). Contributions must be written in English and report on original, unpublished work, not submitted for publication elsewhere. Accepted papers will be included (with authors consent) in indexed workshop proceedings, to be published in a new IEEE open access journal (IEEE transactions on Quantum Engineering).

Due to space restrictions, participation in this workshop will be limited.
